Moleclar mechanism of positive-strand RNA viral replication

Positive-strand (+) RNA virus is one of the most abundant virus on the planet. Though their genetic informations are quite distinct, they share some common features in genome replication, where a virus-induced membrane rearrangement forms to protect viral replication process from host immunosystem. The viral RNA replication is regulated by viral proteins that assemble a nanomachnie on the membrane and the ultrastructural similarity among (+)RNA viruses makes it a potential target for a broader antiviral development, including ZIKA virus, West Nile virus, Yellow Fever virus, Hapetitis C virus and SARS-cov-2 coronavirus that is the cause of the COVID-19 pandemic.

December 2014 - April 2018

Postdoctoral research fellow
Multiscale imaging study of voltage-dependent Ca2+ channels in the regulation synaptic of neurotransmitter release properties.

Synaptic plasticity is crucial to modulate sophisticate functions in neuronal circuits in our brain. A conserved molecular mechanism underlies synaptic functions is involved in hierarchical interaction through proteins at highly specified compartments in nerve cells, known as active zone. Upon arrival of a single action potential at the presynaptic terminal, only a few voltage-gated calcium channels (VGCC) opens at the presynaptic terminal and subsequent rise of intracellular calcium concentration through the VGCC triggers the synaptic transmission by releasing neurotransmitters containing vesicles that are fused to the presynaptic membrane. Therefore, efficient synaptic transmission demands a spatial and temporal precision of VGCC distribution at the presynaptic active zone and altered VGCC localization is related to synaptic disorders. Yet, how VGCCs are localized and genes involved in regulating VGCC distributions are still enigmatic and need to be studied. By applying high-pressure freezing/freeze-substitution, electron tomography and super-resolution light microscopy, I aim to study molecular machinary at the presynaptic active zone that regulates neurotransmitter release probability and synaptic plasticity.

2010 - 2014

Ph.D. Degree
Ph.D. in Neurobiology and Biophysics

Université de Pierre et Marie Curie, Paris, France

Voltage-dependent calcium channels (VDCC) are key actors for the triggering of neurotransmitter release in response to neuronal depolarization. At chemical synapses, the depolarization of presynaptic plasma membrane causes the opening of a small number of VDCCs at the active zone. The subsequent rise of intracellular Ca2+ that can trigger the fusion of synaptic vesicles (SV) with the plasma membrane is restricted within tens of nanometers around VDCCs. Fast and efficient synaptic transmissions appear to be tightly coupled with the relative distribution between the VDCCs and synaptic vesicles fusion sites. However, the connection between VDCCs and docked SVs at a few nanometer scales remains partly enigmatic. To localize VDCCs in 3D with a nanometer resolution I implanted a novel approach combining in vivo labeling of VDCCs via genetically-encoded extracellular epitope tags and electron microscopy. In C. elegans, the neuronal VDCC is composed of a pore forming subunit α1/UNC-2, an auxiliary subunit α2-δ/UNC-36 and an intracellular subunit β. However, the poor affinity of antibodies against low-copy VDCCs and their limited access to narrow synaptic regions are major challenges for high resolution detection of VDCCs in C. elegans. I engineered a VDCC α2-δ subunit /UNC-36 that contains a GFP tag at the N-terminal extracellular domain; UNC-36 has been shown associated with both the neuronal (named UNC-2) and the muscular (named EGL-19) VDCC α1 subunit. Thus, specific tagging of UNC-36 provides means to investigate both neuronal and muscular VDCC localization. I then used a homemade multishell Cadmium based quantum dot (QD) as both fluorescent and electron dense probe. For high-resolution VDCC distribution analysis at C. elegans neuromuscular junction (NMJ), I performed in vivo labeling by QD conjugated antibody injection and correlative electron microscopy. In parallel, I pursued the development of techniques to track individual VDCC with nanometer scale precision in live worms by in vivo complementation activated light microscopy with self-complemented split GFP proteins. We discovered that VDCCs diffuse at sarcomeric membrane within nanodomains and their nanoscale diffusion behavior is modulated in response to muscle tension. In addition, we found that dystrophin participates in electro-mechanical coupling at the sarcolemma by modulating the confinement size of VDCCs. Meanwhile at NMJs, we discovered that lateral diffusion behaviors of N-type VDCC reveal three different populations, and that RIM/UNC-10 seems involved in regulation of VDCC diffusion behavior via its interaction with VDCC and SVs.

Bioconversion de lignocelluloses à bioéthanol par Designer Protein. La bioconversion de biomasse à bioéthanol a déjà été faite plusieurs années. Le plus souvent utilisé matériel pour la conversion se concerne la première génération. La fermentation de blé et de maïs était la majoritaire stratégie dans la fabrication de bioéthanol industriel. Le coût de baril de pétrole sera plus en plus élevé dans l’avenir, donc le mélange de bioéthanol à l’essence est capable de réduire la situation de diminution de ressource de fossile fuel. Le bioéthanol est un carburant renouvelable et il a un meilleur bilan énergétique et carbonique. Les matériaux lignocellulosiques sont la plus large source dans biosphère, 50% de biomasses sont lignocelluloses. En marché d’États-Unis, la teneur de bioéthanol dans l’essence est arrivée à 30% en 2009. En France, le fabriquant de Chevrolet a annoncé qu’il allait fabriquer la nouvelle voiture qui utilise le mélange de bioéthanol de 60% à 85% maximum (E85). Ce type de l’essence mélange va améliorer le fonctionnement de moteur de voiture. Maintenant, la conversion de biomasse à bioéthanol est à partir de céréales et betteraves qui sont les sources agronomiques. Elles ont la compétition avec alimentation. Aux États-Unis, l’essai de conversion de paille de maïs à éthanol est en route. La recherche de cellulosome chez les anaérobies a commencé au début de 1980s. Ce complexe de multienzymatique système est plus en plus intéressant pour l’industrie. La technique s’appelle Designe Protein, on pourra transférer ces gènes codant la cellulosome à E. coli pour produire les fusions protéines chez E. coli qui ont la capacité de faire l’action synergétique comme le cellulosome à dégrader les lignocelluloses. On pourra aussi transférer les gènes codant le cellulosome à la surface de Zymomonas mobilis qui va produire l’éthanol, mais c’est difficile à transformer Zymononas mobilis à reconstruire le minicellulosome stable sur sa surface. C’est aussi difficile à produire dizaines unités enzymatiques différentes et les certaines cohésines et dockerines chez E. coli.
